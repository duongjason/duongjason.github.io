Insitu, Hood River, Oregon

Hardware Electronics Intern

It was here where I learned the ins and outs of the aerospace industry. During my time at Insitu, I worked on multiple projects that directly affected the quality and production of the engine board for one of the unmanned aircraft systems. In the field, it is important that the system operates well under different environmental stresses, including but not limited to factors such as heat, temperature, humidity, and proximity to natural obstacles. Thus, it was crucial that I performed a part stress analysis on different components on the engine board, as one failure could be catastrophic for the aircraft. During the analysis, I characterized different properties such as power dissipation, applied voltages, and derating specifications. By cultivating my attention to detail and handling so many different board components responsibly, I made finding causes of failure much more efficient and gained the trust of my peers when doing rigorous circuit analysis. Here is a photo of an unmanned aircraft system on a launcher, prepared for flight.

In addition to much of the theoretical work done, I also worked with the actual engine board in the laboratory. I performed many different tests to ensure the functionality of the engine board, including continuity and resistivity tests, sensor monitoring, voltage probing, and firmware calibration. In doing so, I developed a functional acceptance test procedure that made it possible for my co-workers and board vendors to reproduce the exact same tests that I performed. This guaranteed consistency when testing the boards and defined the standards that engine boards were expected to operate at after being manufactured. The acceptance test procedure helped expose problems on different engine boards, such as certain voltage rails not being met or parts of a circuit that weren't functioning properly.

To improve the quality of the test setup, I also worked on a project that simulated the engine board circuitry while connected to a fuel pump. I designed and built a circuit that used the engine board as a model, effectively eliminating the need for excess cabling and huge power supplies that would otherwise clutter the lab benches. Instead, it required a small CPU power supply and interfaced with an NI data acquistion system. Using high-frequency analysis, I specified different inductors, resistors, and capacitors required for filtering in the circuit, incorporated design changes to protect the power supply against kickback voltages, and performed a power analysis to ensure the components used were rated properly. In the process, I gained a lot of experience soldering onto perf-boards, working with network connections, and optimizing the cost of all the components required in the system.

When working with engine boards, it is also important to consider the deterioration that occurs after months or years of operation. The wear and tear can cause damage to different components, and the aircraft can go down when least expected. To address this, I drafted a procedure to perform highly accelerated life testing, HALT, on the engine board. This is the process of stressing the board to its destruct limits, points at which all functionality is lost even after returning to operational limits. I included procedures for hot and cold temperature step stresses, vibrational tests, rapid thermal ramps, voltage and current margining, and input signal timing manipulation. Factors that I considered included things such as temperature dwell rate, different times to power cycle, and performing a verification HALT test to validate any solutions proposed.

Mirai IT Solutions, New York, New York

Software Engineering Intern

At Mirai IT Solutions, I learned a lot about Android app development and working with Java object-oriented programming to create a point of sale system. By implementing the model-view-controller (MVC) architectural pattern, I used a broadcast platform to facilitate communication between activities and fragments in the application. This gave the system modularity, making debugging more efficient and the code easier to analyze. In developing the app, I also made considerations regarding the structure of the code and the actual layout of the app. I designed different Java classes with standard naming and commenting conventions, allowing others to easily understand the coding concept and design.

To provide full functionality of the point of sale system, I created a SQL server database and established a reliable connection between the server and the Android application. The database included many different items that were served by the restaurant, and I helped with categorization to make navigation of the system easier. This database held all of the information required by the application, including things such as price, food type, and customer numbers. Because the values for these different categories could change with time, such as the price of food increasing, I programmed the code dynamically. Thus, any changes in the database would automatically be updated in the application, providing an extra layer of separation between the user and the back-end.

I also did a lot of work with front-end development by producing XML files that reflected the design of the written code on an Android device. I changed the attributes of different fragments, laid out different buttons on the screen so that they would be aesthetically pleasing, and included tables that would update upon tapping these buttons on the device. This made the application user-friendly, especially important when used in energetic settings such as restaurants.

An important aspect of the internship was also learning to present my ideas to other people. I gained a lot of experience working with PowerPoint and communicating with others who didn't speak the same language as me. This helped me focus on the important points of my application and create diagrams that explained what I was trying to accomplish with the code despite the language barrier.
